\section{Two definitions of nearby cycles}\label{sect:two_definitions_of_nearby_cycles}

On $\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1$ with coordinate $t$, given any $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$, we have a left indecomposable $\D_{{A}^1_\C}$ module
\[
    K^\alpha_m :=  \frac{\D_{\mathbb{A}^1_\C}}{\D_{\mathbb{A}^1_\C}\cdot(t\partial_t - \alpha)^m}
\]
First note that $t\in K^\alpha_m$ has \textbf{both} a left and a right inverse, i.e., action by multiplying $t$ induce a bijection.
So $K^\alpha_m$ not only a (left, see 2. in Lem.~\ref{lem:quasi-coherent-sheaf-and-localization-of-D-modules}) $\C[t]\subseteq \D_{\mathbb{A}^1_\C}$ module, but also a (left) $\C[t,t^{-1}]$-module that extending the $\C[t]$-module structure.

We take sections $e^\alpha_i := \overline{(t\partial_t - \alpha)^{m-1-i}}$ (equivalence class) then we have a presentation:
\[
    K^\alpha_m = \bigoplus_{i=0}^{m-1} \mathbb{C}[t, t^{-1}] e^\alpha_i
\]
with $\partial_t e^\alpha_i = \frac{1}{t}(\alpha e^\alpha_i + e^\alpha_{i-1})$ for all $i$ (note we have claimed that $t$ is invertible), where $e^\alpha_{-1} = 0$,
and the direct sum notation should be treated as \textbf{internal} direct sum.

Then when restricted to $\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\}$, $K^\alpha_m\mid_{\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\}}$
becomes a \textbf{finitely generated}, (locally) free $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\}}$-module, i.e., an \textbf{integrable connection} (see \hyperref[def:integrable-connection]{definition}).

Then the matrix (note $K^\alpha_m$ is not a vector space since $\C[t,t^{-1}]$ is not a field) of $t\partial_t$-action on $K_m^{\alpha}$ is $J_{\alpha,m}$, the $m\times m$ Jordan block with eigenvalue $\alpha$:
\[
    J_{\alpha,m} = \begin{bmatrix}
        \alpha & 1      &        &        \\
               & \alpha & \ddots &        \\
               &        & \ddots & 1      \\
               &        &        & \alpha
    \end{bmatrix}_{m \times m}
\]

\begin{remark}
    Here $e_\ell^{\alpha}$ can be understood as the formal symbol
    of the multivalued function
    \[
        t^{\alpha} \cdot \frac{(\log t)^\ell}{\ell!}.
    \]
    And $K^\alpha_{m}$ indicates about the section not flat,
    a \textbf{flat section} means the section is in the kernel of connection
    \[
        \nabla:=t\partial_t \in\End_\C(K^\alpha_m).
    \]

    Indeed in \cite[Ch.~5]{hotta2008d},
    they develop arguments or definitions such as ``moderate growth'' by multivalued functions,
    so it is not only as a heuristic symbol to introduce multivalued function.
\end{remark}

Then locally around a simply connected neighborhood (Euclidean topology) of $(\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\})^{\mathrm{an}}$,
with simply connectedness, we obtain that $\mathrm{\log}\, t$ is a well-defined analytic function (so we should consider the analytification of the module $K^\alpha_m$).
Consider
$$
    v_{\ell} := e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t} \cdot e_{\ell}^{\alpha} \in (K^\alpha_m)^{\mathrm{an}}
    = \frac{\D^{\mathrm{an}}_{(\mathbb{A}^1_\C)^\mathrm{an}}}{\D^{\mathrm{an}}_{(\mathbb{A}^1_\C)^\mathrm{an}}\cdot(t\partial_t - \alpha)^m}
    = \bigoplus_{i=0}^{m-1} \mathcal{O}^{\mathrm{an}}_{(\mathbb{A}^1_\C)^\mathrm{an}}\left[\frac{1}{t}\right] e^\alpha_i,
$$
for $\ell = 0, \cdots, m-1$,
we then have, by the Leibniz rule,
\begin{align*}
    t\partial_t \circ \left(e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t} \cdot e_{\ell}^{\alpha}\right)
     & := t\partial_t \cdot e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t} \cdot e_{\ell}^{\alpha}                                                                                                                                                                                             \\
     & = \left[t\partial_t, e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t}\right] \cdot e_{\ell}^{\alpha} + e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t} \cdot t\partial_t \cdot e_{\ell}^{\alpha}                                                                                                                 \\
     & = t\partial_t\left(e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t}\right) \cdot e_{\ell}^{\alpha} + e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t} \cdot t\partial_t \cdot e_{\ell}^{\alpha}                              &  & \text{(see \cite[Prop~2.25(iii)]{mustata2023dmodules})}                         \\
     & = t \left( -J_{\alpha,m} \frac{1}{t} e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t} \right) \cdot e_{\ell}^{\alpha} + e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t} \cdot \left( J_{\alpha,m} e_{\ell}^{\alpha} \right)                                                                                      \\
     & = -J_{\alpha,m} \left( e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t} \cdot e_{\ell}^{\alpha} \right) + J_{\alpha,m} \left( e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t} \cdot e_{\ell}^{\alpha} \right)               &  & \text{($\C$ is in the \textbf{center} of $\D^{\an}_{(\mathbb{A}^1_\C)^{\an}}$)} \\
     & = 0
\end{align*}
we use $\cdot$ to denote the multiplication in $\D^{\an}_{(\mathbb{A}^1_\C)^{\an}}$, and we get $v_{\ell}$ are $t\partial_t$-flat.

\bigskip

We have a local system, recall the equivalence given in Prop.~\ref{prop:classification-of-local-system}:
$$
    L_m^{\lambda}=\left(\bigoplus \limits_{i=0}^{m-1}\mathbb{C}\cdot v_{i}, e^{-2\pi iJ_{\alpha,m}}\right)
$$
There are several things to be point out:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Taking fiber (not stalk) at any given point $p\in(\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\})^{\mathrm{an}}$, $\Ker(\nabla)$ becomes a finite ($m$) dimensional $\C$ vector space.
    \item On a simply connected Euclidean open set $p \in U \subset (\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\})^{\mathrm{an}}$, we can give $\log\, t(p)$ a concreate $\C$-value.
    \item View $e^\alpha_i(p)$ as the canonical basis vector (the $i+1$-th entry is $1$, others are $0$)
          \[
              \begin{pmatrix}
                  0 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 0
              \end{pmatrix}\in\C^m,
          \]
          where the $1$ is at the $(i+1)$-th row. Then we identify
          \[
              v^\alpha_i(p) = e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t}(p) \cdot e^\alpha_i(p)
          \]
          with
          \[
              e^{-J_{\alpha,m} \log t}(p) \cdot \begin{pmatrix}
                  0 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 0
              \end{pmatrix} \in \C^m.
          \]
    \item So the monodromy action (of a generator of $\pi_1((\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\})^{\mathrm{an}}, p)$) acts on $\C^m$ by left multiplication with $e^{-2\pi i J_{\alpha,m}}$.
\end{enumerate}
We denote $\lambda = e^{-2\pi i \alpha}\in \C$.

\bigskip

Since $K^\alpha_m\mid_{\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\}}$ is an integrable connection on $\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\}$ (note that $\C[t, t^{-1}]$ is also smooth finite type over $\C$), recall by Riemann-Hilbert correspondence Thm.~\ref{thm:basic-RH-corr}, we have \cite[Thm.~7.2.1]{hotta2008d} that in $\D^b(\underline{\C}_{X^\mathrm{an}})$,
$$
    \text{DR}\left((K_m^{\alpha}\mid_{\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\}})^{\mathrm{an}}\right) \xrightarrow{\substack{\text{quasi-isomorphic}\\ \sim}}  L_m^{\lambda}.
$$
Since the direct system (in category of coherent (finitely generated) $\D_{\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1}$-modules)
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{array}{ccccccc}
        K_{m-1}^{\alpha} & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & K_m^{\alpha}                              & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & K_{m+1}^{\alpha}                            & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & \cdots \\[1.5ex]
        \overline P      & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & \overline{P \cdot (t\partial_t - \alpha)} & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & \overline{P \cdot (t\partial_t - \alpha)^2} & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & \cdots
    \end{array}
\end{equation*}
induces a direct system of local systems
(categorical equivalence is given by functors, so it also transports morphisms),
arrows should be viewed as $\pi_1((\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\})^\mathrm{an}, p)$-\textbf{equivariant} $\C$-linear maps by Prop.~\ref{prop:classification-of-local-system}:
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{array}{ccccccc}
        L_{m-1}^{\lambda}=\bigoplus \limits_{i=0}^{m-2}\C\cdot v_i & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & L_m^{\lambda}=\bigoplus \limits_{i=0}^{m-1}\C\cdot v_i & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & L_{m+1}^{\lambda}=\bigoplus \limits_{i=0}^{m}\C\cdot v_i & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & \cdots \\[1.5ex]
        v_i                                                        & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & v_i                                                    & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & v_i                                                      & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & \cdots
    \end{array}
\end{equation*}
To see why the morphism are $\pi_1((\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\})^\mathrm{an}, p)$-equivariant,
since $J_{\alpha, m}$ is an upper-triangular matrix, then $e^{-2\pi iJ_{\alpha,m}}$ is also upper-triangular, so each $L^\lambda_{i}\subseteq L^\lambda_{i+1}$ is a sub $\pi_1((\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\})^\mathrm{an}, p)$-representation.

Let $X = \text{Spec } R$ be a smooth affine algebraic variety over $\mathbb{C}$, and $f \in R$,
a regular function, we have the following commutative diagram:
\begin{center}\phantomsection\label{commutative_diagram_of_hyper_surface}
    \begin{tikzcd}
        {(f=0)\atop \text{divisor}} & X && {\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}} \\
        \\
        & {U=X\backslash(f=0)} && {\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash \{0\}}
        \arrow["i", hook, from=1-1, to=1-2]
        \arrow["f", from=1-2, to=1-4]
        \arrow["j", hook', from=3-2, to=1-2]
        \arrow["f", from=3-2, to=3-4]
        \arrow[hook', from=3-4, to=1-4]
    \end{tikzcd}
\end{center}
where the morphism $f$ is given by the ring map:
\begin{align*}
    \C[t] & \xrightarrow{\quad} R                       \\
    \C    & \xrightarrow{\mathrm{id}_\C} \C \subseteq R \\
    t     & \xmapsto{\quad} f
\end{align*}
And also note that the locus of $f$ (as an \textbf{analytic set} in complex manifold $X^\mathrm{an}$) can be \textbf{non-smooth}.

We define
$$
    \Psi_{f,\lambda} = \lim_{\substack{\longrightarrow \\ m \to \infty}} i^{-1} Rj_\ast L_m^{\lambda}
$$
which is called the $\lambda\textbf{-nearby cycles}$ of $f$.
And $T$-action ($\pi_1((\mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1 \setminus \{0\})^\mathrm{an})$-action) on $L_m^{\lambda}$ induces $T$-action on $\Psi_{f,\lambda}$.



\begin{remark}
    Firstly, note that since $\{f=0\}$ may not smooth, so $i^{-1}$ is the sheaf-theoretic pull-back between topological (ringed) spaces.
    Which is somewhat different from scheme case.

    Secondly, $j_{\ast}$ is the direct image functor,
    and it's a left exact functor,
    we write $Rj_\ast$ the right derived functor of $j_{\ast}$,
    which can be viewed as the complex of sheaves obtained by taking the \textbf{injective resolution} of $L_{m}^{\lambda}$ and applying $j_{\ast}$.
    Since $j_\ast$ maybe have nontrivial cohomology even in a non-$\D$-module-theoretic context, we choose to use a derived functor.
\end{remark}

The above definition is a topological definition, which is a bit differed from Deligne's. And total cycles is defined by combining all information from nearby cycles.


On the other hand, recall Example~\ref{Example 8.11}, we have
$$
    \mathcal{D}_{X}[s]f^{s} \subseteq R\left[\frac{1}{f}\right][s] \cdot f^{s}.
$$

For closed point $\alpha \in \mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^{1} \simeq \operatorname{Spec} \mathbb{C}[s]$, we use $\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}$ to denote its maximal ideal. We have proved in Example \ref{Example 8.11}:

\begin{remark}\label{rmk:justify_R_f[s]f^s}
    We now justify the symbol $R_f[s]f^s$,
    since in Rmk.~\ref{remark:second_treatment_of_example_8.11} we only get the module $R_f[\partial_t]$,
    and $f^s$ represent the canonical section $1\otimes 1$.

    One should note that the notation $R_f[\partial_t]$ (or more precisely $\C[\partial_t]\otimes_\C R_f$) itself doesn't imply any module structure.
    If we want to make it an $R_f$ (left or right) module, we should induce the action from its left $\D_Y$-module structure.

    The Step 5 Block A of Rmk.~\ref{remark:second_treatment_of_example_8.11} gives us an answer.
    We have a projection
    \[
        p: Y = X\times_{\Spec \C}\mathbb{A}^1_{\C} \to X
    \]
    which makes $\Ox$ a subring of $\Oy$.
    So we get a left $R$ module structure by
    \[
        R=\Ox\xhookrightarrow{p^\sharp} \Oy \subseteq \D_Y.
    \]

    Since before we pushforward left $\D_X$-module $R_f$ along $\Gamma_f$,
    $f\in\D_X$ already acts bijectively (left and right) on $R_f$,
    which makes $t\in\D_Y$ act bijectively on $\Gamma_{f,+}(R_f) = \C[\partial_t] \otimes_{\C} R_f$ \cite[Lemma~2.1.5]{PopaDMBG2021}.
    Then by Step 5 Block A+D of Rmk.~\ref{remark:second_treatment_of_example_8.11},
    we discover $f\in R$ also act bijectively on $\Gamma_{f,+}(R_f)$.
    So we finally give a left $R_f$ module structure on $\C[\partial_t] \otimes_{\C} R_f$.

    Then we discover $s = -t\partial_t$ and $R$ commute in $\D_Y$, this is because we have
    \[
        [\partial_t, g] = \partial_t(g) = 0 \quad (\text{see  \cite[Prop~2.25(iii)]{mustata2023dmodules}})
    \]
    By universal property of polynomial ring, we have $R_f[s]$ embedded in $\D_Y$ (as a $\C$-subalgebra),
    if we map formal symbol $s$ to $-t\partial_t \in \D_Y$.

    \medskip

    Then we can act $R_f[s]$ onto $1\otimes 1 \in \Gamma_{f,+}(R_f)$, then we claim:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item this action is faithful, so we get a free rank 1 $R_f[s]$ submodule in $\Gamma_{f,+}(R_f)$.
        \item This submodule is the whole of $\Gamma_{f,+}(R_f)$.
    \end{enumerate}
    We denote it as $R_f[s]f^s$.
    Then we can give the abstractly constructed $R_f[s]$ a left $\D_Y$ module structure by pulling back the one on $\Gamma_{f,+}(R_f)$.
\end{remark}

\bigskip

\begin{remark}\label{rmk:base_change_of_R_f[s]_f^s}
    We then try to define $R_f(s)f^s$ and $R_f[s]_{\frak{m}_\alpha}f^s$, the construction should satisfy:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item From (the proof in) the corollary~\ref{cor:bernstein_beilinson} below, which suggests that this construction should be functorial.
        \item Be consistent with \cite[The $\D_{U_s}$ module $\mathcal{M}$ defined in Proof of Theorem 2.2.1.]{PopaDMBG2021}.
    \end{enumerate}

    This is equivalent to say that we have a ring ($\C$-algebra) map
    \[
        \D_Y \to \End_\C(R_f[s])
    \]
    And we have a composition
    \[
        \D_X\otimes_\C \C[s] \to \D_Y \to \End_\C(R_f[s])
    \]
    by assigning $s$ to $-t\partial_t\in\D_Y$

    \medskip

    We define $R_f(s) := R_f[s]\otimes_{\C[s]}\C(s)$ and $R_f[s]_{\frak{m}_\alpha} := R_f[s]\otimes_{\C[s]}\C[s]_{\frak{m}_\alpha}$.
    Before giving it a left $\D$ module structure, we discover that this construction is functorial (in the category of $\C[s]$-modules).

    \medskip

    Then we give it a left $\D$ module structure, this structure should also be functorial.
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $R_f(s)$ should have a left $\D_{R(s)/\C(s)} = \D_{R/\C}\otimes_\C \C[s]\otimes_{\C[s]}\C(s)$ module structure, see Lem.~\ref{lem:base_change_of_D_modules}.
        \item $R_f[s]_{\frak{m}_\alpha}$ should have a left $\D_{R[s]_{\frak{m}_\alpha}/\C[s]_{\frak{m}_\alpha}} = \D_{R/\C}[s]\otimes_{\C[s]} \C[s]_{\frak{m}_\alpha}$ module structure, but one should note $\C[s]_{\frak{m}_\alpha}$ is not a field.
    \end{enumerate}
    Thanks to the construction in the proof in Lem.~\ref{lem:base_change_of_D_modules}, the base change of the \textbf{ring of \underline{relative} differential operator} is functorial.

    \medskip

    To give a (left) module structure is equivalent to give the ring map
    \begin{align*}
        \D_{R/\C}[s]\otimes_{\C[s]}\C(s) & \to \End_\C(R_f[s]\otimes_{\C[s]}\C(s))                    \\
        P \otimes a                      & \mapsto \left( u \otimes b \mapsto P(u) \otimes ab \right)
    \end{align*}
    and
    \begin{align*}
        \D_{R/\C}[s]\otimes_{\C[s]}\C[s]_{\frak{m}_\alpha} & \to \End_\C(R_f[s]\otimes_{\C[s]}\C[s]_{\frak{m}_\alpha})  \\
        P \otimes a                                        & \mapsto \left( u \otimes b \mapsto P(u) \otimes ab \right)
    \end{align*}
    From the two formulas above, one see this definition of ring action (on module) is functorial. Where $u\mapsto P(u)$ is just
    \[
        \D_{R/\C} \otimes_\C \C[s] = \D_X\otimes_\C \C[s] \to \D_Y \to \End_\C(R_f[s])
    \]
    mentioned above.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:existence_of_b_function}
    There exists $b(s) \in \mathbb{C}[s]$ s.t.
    \[
        b(s) \quad \mathrm{ kills } \quad \frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]f^{s}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]f^{s+1}}
    \]
    see Thm.~\ref{Theorem 8.4} or Ex.~\ref{Example 8.11} or Rmk.~\ref{remark:second_treatment_of_example_8.11}.
\end{theorem}



\begin{corollary}[Bernstein-Beilinson]\label{cor:bernstein_beilinson}
    Let $\mathbb{C}(s)$ be the fractional field of $\mathbb{C}[s]$, and $\mathcal{D}_{X}(s) = \mathcal{D}_{X} \otimes_{\mathbb{C}} \mathbb{C}(s)$; for all $\alpha \in \mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^{1}$ closed point, we obtain that:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $\mathcal{D}_{X}(s) f^{s+k} \simeq \mathcal{D}_{X}(s) f^{s}, $ for all $  k \in \mathbb{Z}$.
        \item There exists $k_{0} \geqslant 0$ s.t.
              \begin{itemize}
                  \item $\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s-k} = \mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s-k_{0}}, $ for all $ k > k_{0}$
                  \item $\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s+k} = \mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s+k_{0}},$ for all $  k > k_{0}$
              \end{itemize}
    \end{enumerate}

\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}\label{rmk:D_X(s)f^s=R_f(s)f^s_and_D_X[s]_m_alpha_f^s=R_f[s]_m_alpha_f^s}
    Noted that here  $\mathcal{D}_{X}(s) f^{s} = R[\frac{1}{f}](s) f^{s},\ \mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s} = R[\frac{1}{f}][s]_{m_{\alpha}} f^{s}$.
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
    \mbox{}

    $1)$ Since $\mathbb{C}(s)$ can be view as the localization at $(0)$ of $\mathbb{C}[s]$,
    we have $\mathbb{C}(s)$ is flat over $\mathbb{C}[s]$,
    thus
    \[
        \frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]f^{s}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]f^{s+1}}\otimes \mathbb{C}(s)=\frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}(s)f^{s}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}(s)f^{s+1}}
    \]
    Note that
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item To commute tensor product with quotient, the ring map $\mathbb{C}[s]\to \mathbb{C}(s)$ is not needed to be flat, all tensor product is right exact.
        \item To make the incuced $\D_X(s)$-structure the same on LHS and RHS, the $\D_X(s)$-structure should be functorial, which is required in Rmk.~\ref{rmk:base_change_of_R_f[s]_f^s}.
    \end{enumerate}

    From the $\D_X(s)$-structure mentioned in Rmk.~\ref{rmk:base_change_of_R_f[s]_f^s}, we have
    \[
        b(s+k) \quad\mathrm{kills} \quad\frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}(s)f^{s}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}(s)f^{s+1}} \implies \D_{R/\C}[s]\otimes_{\C[s]} \C(s) \ni b(s+k)\otimes 1  \quad\mathrm{kills } \quad\frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]f^{s}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]f^{s+1}} \otimes_{\C[s]} \C(s)
    \]
    And $b(s+k) \neq 0$ is invertible in $\mathbb{C}(s)$, so
    \[
        b(s+k) \quad\mathrm{kills} \quad\frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}(s)f^{s}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}(s)f^{s+1}} \implies \frac{\mathcal{D}_X(s) f^{s+k}}{\mathcal{D}_X(s) f^{s+k-1}} = 0.
    \]

    $2)$  Note that Bernstein-Sato polynomial $b(s)\in\D_X[s]$ has coefficients in $\C$, see Thm.~\ref{Theorem 8.4}.

    Since the number of roots of $b(s)$ is finite,
    there exists $k_0 \ge 0$, $b(\alpha \pm k) \neq 0 \in\mathbb{C}$ for all $k > k_0$,
    then $b(s \pm k) \notin \mathfrak{m}_\alpha$ for all $k > k_0$.

    So $b(s \pm k)$ is invertible in $\mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha}$ for all $k > k_0$,
    similarly, we get
    \[
        \frac{\mathcal{D}_X[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} f^{s \pm k}}{\mathcal{D}_X[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} f^{s \pm k + 1}} = 0.
    \]
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}
    For all $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $\mathcal{D}_X[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} f^{s+k},  k \gg 0$ is stabled, called the $\textbf{minimal extension}$ of $R[\frac{1}{f}][s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} f^s$.
        \item $\Psi_{f,\alpha} := \frac{\mathcal{D}_X[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} f^{s-k}}{\mathcal{D}_X[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} f^{s+k}}$ is called the $\alpha\textbf{-nearby cycles}$ of $R$ along $f$.

              \cite[Definition~3.3.2.]{PopaDMBG2021} defines that $\alpha$-nearby cycles is a $\mathcal{D}_X$-module, NOT a $\mathcal{D}_X[s]$-module.
        \item $\Lambda := \mathbb{Z} + \{\text{roots of } b_{f}(s)\}$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
    For one wonder why here we \emph{define} the minimal extension,
    see \cite[Theorem~2.10]{Wu2020ComparisonNearbyCyclesBfunctions}, which use
    \cite[Def~3.4.1]{hotta2008d} to define the minimal extension and claim the equivalence of the two.

    In $\mathcal{D}$-module theory,
    an extension is usually about given a module over an open subset $U$,
    and extending it to the whole space $X$ to get a $\mathcal{D}_{X}$-module,
    and we shall pay attention on how it extends around $f=0$.
    Take $U=\mathrm{Spec}\, R_{f}$,
    if the module over $U=X\backslash(f=0)$ can be viewed as $R$-module,
    then it's an extension,
    and we call $R_{f}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s}$  $\textbf{maximal extension}$.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:properties_of_alpha_nearby_cycles}
    \quad

    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $s - \alpha$ acts on $\Psi_{f,\alpha}$ nilpotently
        \item $\Psi_{f,\alpha} \simeq \Psi_{f,\alpha+1}$ (Note: a $\D_X$-module homomorphism, not a $\D_X[s]$-module homomorphism)
        \item $\Psi_{f,\alpha} \neq 0$ if and only if $\alpha \in \Lambda$
        \item $\Psi_{f,\alpha}$ is a regular holonomic $D_X$-module.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
    \mbox{}

    \begin{enumerate}
        \item The descending chain of submodules
              \[
                  \mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s+k_{0}}\subseteq\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s+k_{0}-1}\subseteq\dots\subseteq \mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s-k_{0}}
              \]
              induces a finite filtration on the quotient module
              \[
                  \frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s-k_{0}}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s+k_{0}}},
              \]
              whose successive quotients are of the form
              \[
                  \frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s+i}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s+i+1}}.
              \]
              For each such quotient, there exists a polynomial $b_{i}(s)$ that annihilates it (before taking localization). Because $s-\alpha$ is the only non-invertible irreducible polynomial in the local ring $\mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}$, each $b_{i}(s)$ takes the form $u_{i}(s-\alpha)^{n_{i}}$ for some unit $u_{i} \in \mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}$.
              By taking $n = \sum n_{i}$, the element $(s-\alpha)^{n}$ annihilates the entire module $\frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s-k_{0}}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s+k_{0}}}$. Hence, $s-\alpha$ acts nilpotently on it.

        \item Recall that $t$ acts on $R_f[s]f^s$ bijectively. Consider the $\mathcal{D}_{X}$-linear automorphism of $\mathcal{D}_{X}(s)f^{s}$ defined by the shift
              \[
                  T_{-1}\big(P(s)f^{s}\big) = P(s-1)f^{s-1}.
              \]
              For any element $m$, we have
              \[
                  T_{-1}(s \cdot m) = (s-1) \cdot T_{-1}(m), \quad\text{and generally}\quad T_{-1}(P(s) \cdot m) = P(s-1) \cdot T_{-1}(m).
              \]
              This means $T_{-1}$ is not $\D_X[s]$-linear.

              When localizing at $\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}$,
              a polynomial $g(s)$ becomes invertible if $g(\alpha) \neq 0$.
              Under the shift, the action of $g(s)$ becomes the action of $g(s-1)$, evaluated at $s = \alpha+1$ is also non-zero, making it invertible in $\mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha+1}}$.
              So we can safely define the map below:
              \begin{align*}
                  T_{-1}: \mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s-k} & \stackrel{\sim}{\longrightarrow} \mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha+1}}f^{s-k-1} \\
                  \frac{m}{g(s)}                                            & \longmapsto \frac{T_{-1}(m)}{g(s-1)}
              \end{align*}
              for any integer $k$. And one should verify that it is indeed an isomorphism.

              For $k \gg 0$, the sequence of modules stabilizes. Passing to the respective quotients, this induces the desired $\mathcal{D}_X$-module isomorphism $\Psi_{f,\alpha} \xrightarrow{\sim} \Psi_{f,\alpha+1}$.

        \item Since $\alpha \not\in\Lambda$, $\alpha$ is not a root of $b(s+k)$, then $b(s+k)$ is a unit for all $k$, thus $\Psi_{f,\alpha}=0$.

              If $\Psi_{f,\alpha}\neq 0$,
              there exists $i$ such that $\frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s+i}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s+i+1}}\neq 0$,
              and then $\alpha$ is a root of $b(s+i)$ such that $b(s+i)$ is not invertible in $\mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}$. Thus $\alpha\in\Lambda$.

        \item $M_{\alpha} = \mathbb{C}[t, \frac{1}{t}] \cdot t^{\alpha}$ (see Ex.~\ref{ex:first-examples-of-d-modules}) is regular
              (at least analytically at the stalk of $0$, see Rmk.~\ref{rmk:indecomposable_objects_in_RH_D} and Prop.~\ref{prop:classification-unipotent-indecomposable-regular-holonomic},
              to be verified algebraically it should be embedded into $\mathbb{P}^1_\C$, see Rmk.~\ref{rmk:algebraic_KK_regularity_holomonic})
              holonomic $\mathcal{D}_{\mathbb{A}^{1}_{\mathbb{C}}}$-module.

              Consider
              \begin{align*}
                  f: X   & \longrightarrow \mathbb{A}^{1}_{\mathbb{C}} \\
                  \alpha & \longmapsto t=f(\alpha)
              \end{align*}
              which is induced by
              \begin{align*}
                  \mathbb{C}[t] & \longrightarrow R \\
                  t             & \longmapsto f
              \end{align*}
              We admit the fact that the $\D$-module theoretical (derived) \textbf{inverse image} functor \cite[Sect.~1.5, It is the derived one that comptible with Thm.~6.1.5]{hotta2008d} preserves (algebraic we use here) regular holonomicity \cite[Thm.~6.1.5]{hotta2008d}, then
              \[
                  f^{*} M_{\alpha}:=M_{\alpha}\otimes_{\mathbb{C}[t]} R\simeq R_{f}\cdot f^\alpha= R\left[\frac{1}{f}\right] f^{\alpha}
              \]
              makes $R_{f}\cdot f^\alpha$ a regular holonomic $\mathcal{D}_{X}$-module.

              With $(2)$, we can assume $\alpha\neq 0$, otherwise, we just shifts $\alpha$ to $\alpha+1$.
              We have a short exact sequence:
              \[
                  0 \longrightarrow \frac{R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}{(s-\alpha) R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}} \xrightarrow{\;(s-\alpha)\cdot\;} \frac{R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}{(s-\alpha)^2 R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}} \xrightarrow{\;\text{proj}\;} \frac{R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}{(s-\alpha) R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}} \longrightarrow 0
              \]
              where the injective mapping is given by \textbf{left} multiplication by $(s-\alpha)$ (i.e., sending the residue class $\overline{m}$ to $\overline{(s-\alpha)m}$), and the surjective mapping is the natural canonical projection.

              Here, the isomorphism
              \[
                  \frac{R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}{(s-\alpha) R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}} \simeq R_{f} \cdot f^{\alpha}
              \]
              arises because quotienting out the $(s-\alpha)$-multiples algebraically corresponds to taking the evaluation map $s \mapsto \alpha$.
              \begin{enumerate}
                  \item The ring $\mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}$ is a local ring with (its unique) maximal ideal $(s-\alpha)$ reduces canonically to $\mathbb{C}$, collapsing the coefficients from $R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}$ down to $R_{f}$.
                  \item And the formal symbol $f^{s}$ becomes $f^{\alpha}$, this step didn't actually do evaulation, since they are \textbf{both} the canonical section $1\otimes 1$ in their respective presentations. So the real thing goes:
                        \[
                            1\otimes 1 \mapsto 1\otimes 1.
                        \]
              \end{enumerate}
              Thus, this quotient module is precisely the regular holonomic $\mathcal{D}_X$-module $R_{f} \cdot f^{\alpha}$.

              Since regular holonomic $\mathcal{D}_{X}$-modules form an abelian category \cite[Combine Prop.~3.1.2 and Def.~6.1.1]{hotta2008d}, we have $\frac{R_{f}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s}}{(s-\alpha)^{2}R_{f}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s}}$ is regular holonomic.

              And further, we obtain the generalized short exact sequence:
              \[
                  0 \longrightarrow \frac{R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}{(s-\alpha) R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}} \xrightarrow{\;(s-\alpha)^N\cdot\;} \frac{R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}{(s-\alpha)^{N+1} R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}} \xrightarrow{\;\text{proj}\;} \frac{R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}{(s-\alpha)^N R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}} \longrightarrow 0
              \]
              By induction, $\frac{R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}{(s-\alpha)^{N} R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}$ is regular holonomic for all $N$.

              By $(1)$, the action of $(s-\alpha)$ on $\Psi_{f,\alpha} = \frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s-k}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s+k}}$ is nilpotent, meaning there exists some large enough integer $N \gg 0$ such that $(s-\alpha)^N$ entirely annihilates $\Psi_{f,\alpha}$.
              Because of this annihilation, the natural surjective $\mathcal{D}_X[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}$-module morphism
              \[
                  \mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s-k} \xrightarrow{\;\text{proj}\;} \frac{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s-k}}{\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s+k}} = \Psi_{f,\alpha}
              \]
              factors through the quotient by the submodule generated by $(s-\alpha)^N$. This explicitly means that the map descends to a well-defined surjective homomorphism:
              \[
                  \frac{\mathcal{D}_X[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s-k}}{(s-\alpha)^N \mathcal{D}_X[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s-k}} \twoheadrightarrow \Psi_{f,\alpha}.
              \]
              Since one can verify
              \begin{enumerate}
                  \item $\mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s-k} \simeq \mathcal{D}_{X}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}}f^{s}$, even in the case one is a proper subset of the other, since then the isomorphism will not be the inclusion.
                  \item Rmk.~\ref{rmk:D_X(s)f^s=R_f(s)f^s_and_D_X[s]_m_alpha_f^s=R_f[s]_m_alpha_f^s},
                        i.e.,
                        $\mathcal{D}_X[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}$ as a submodule $R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}$ actually equals it.
              \end{enumerate}
              Thus, we can view $\Psi_{f,\alpha}$ as a quotient of the $\mathcal{D}_{X}$-module $\frac{R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}{(s-\alpha)^N R_f[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_{\alpha}} f^{s}}$.
              So $\Psi_{f,\alpha}$ itself must also be a regular holonomic $\mathcal{D}_{X}$-module.
    \end{enumerate}

\end{proof}


\section{Comparison theorem}
Our next goal is to prove the following theorem:
\begin{theorem}[Comparison] \label{Theorem 9.5}
    For all $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$, $\lambda = e^{2\pi i\alpha}$,
    $$
        \mathrm{DR}(\Psi_{f,-\alpha}) \simeq i_* \Psi_{f,\lambda}[-1]
    $$
    such that $e^{2\pi is} = \lambda^{-1}\cdot e^{2\pi i(s+\alpha)}$ is the corresponding $T$-action on $\Psi_{f,\lambda}$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{remark}
    The actual definition of a (analytic/topological) \textbf{nearby cycle} will induce a $\mathbb{Z} = \pi_1{\C^*}$ action on $\Psi_{f,\lambda}$, denoted by $T$, see \cite[\S~3.1]{Wu2017NearbyVanishingCycles}.
\end{remark}
\begin{remark}
    This section $\lambda = e^{2\pi i \alpha}$ is different with the previous section Sect.~\ref{sect:two_definitions_of_nearby_cycles} definition $\lambda = e^{-2\pi i \alpha}$,
    in subsequent proofs, we will see this ``error'' cancel out itself.
\end{remark}
\begin{remark}
    With $(s-\alpha)$ acts on $\Psi_{f,\alpha}$ nilpotently, (the action of) $e^{2\pi i(s+\alpha)}$ is essentially a finite sum, hence it is a well-defined operator for $\Psi_{f,\alpha}$.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}\label{def:N_m_alpha_k}
    Recall we have a graph embedding~\ref{def:graph_embedding} $\Gamma_f: X \hookrightarrow Y = X\times_{\Spec \C} \mathbb{A}^1_\C$,
    we define $\D_Y$-module $\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k}$ by several steps:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item We denote $j: U = X \setminus (f=0) \hookrightarrow X$ be the affine open inclusion, $\mathcal{O}_U = R_f$,
              Open embedding makes $j_+$ reduce to $Rj_*$;
              Affine-ness of $j$ makes $Rj_*$ reduce to $j_*$ \cite[Sect.~3.4]{hotta2008d};
              Using Ex.~\ref{example:direct_image_of_graph_embedding}, we get $\Gamma_{f,+}(j_*(\mathcal{O}_U)) = R_f[s]f^s$ a $\D_Y$-module.

        \item Define projection $p_2: Y \to \mathbb{A}^1_\C$ be the projection to the second factor.
              Recall in we have defined $\D_{\mathbb{A}^1_\C}$-module $K^\alpha_m$ in the begging of Sect.~\ref{sect:two_definitions_of_nearby_cycles},
              through the pullback $p_2^*$ (automatically non-derived \cite[Prop.~1.5.13]{hotta2008d}),
              we get $p_2^* K_m^{\alpha}$ a $\D_Y$-module.
              By the isomorphism under \cite[Def.~1.3.1]{hotta2008d},
              says $\D$-module theoretic inverse image is the same with the sheaf theoretic one,
              we get
              \[
                  p_2^* K_m^{\alpha} = R[t] \otimes_{\C[t]} K_m^{\alpha} = \bigoplus_{i=0}^{m-1} R[t, t^{-1}] e^\alpha_i.
              \]
              And the left $\D_Y$-module structure of $p_2^* K_m^{\alpha}$ is explicitly given by
              \[
                  \theta(\psi \otimes s) = \theta(\psi) \otimes s + \psi \cdot \theta(t) \otimes \partial_t s
              \]
              Where $\theta \in \mathcal{T}_Y\subset\D_Y$,
              section $\psi \in \mathcal{O}_Y = R[t]$, and $s \in K_m^{-\alpha}$, using the fact that $t$ is the coordinate of $\mathbb{A}^1_\C$ and $p_2^*(t) = t$.

        \item Then we apply \cite[Proposition~1.2.9.(i)]{hotta2008d},
              get a (left) $\D_Y$-module
              \[
                  \Gamma_{f,+}(j_*(\mathcal{O}_U)) \otimes_{\Oy} p_2^* K_m^{\alpha} = R_f[s]f^s \otimes_{R[t]} \bigoplus_{i=0}^{m-1} R[t, t^{-1}] e^\alpha_i
              \]
              Recall the left $\Oy$-mudule structure of $R_f[s]f^s$ is given be the inclusion $\Oy \hookrightarrow \D_Y$,
              here we use it as right $\Oy$-module structure.

        \item Recall $\D_X[s]\subset \D_Y$ is a $\C$-subalgebra, then we define the $\D_X[s]$-submodule, see \cite[Sect.~3]{Wu2020ComparisonNearbyCyclesBfunctions}
              \[
                  \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k}\subset \Gamma_{f,+}(j_*(\mathcal{O}_U)) \otimes_{\Oy} p_2^* K_m^{\alpha}
              \]
              generated be sections $f^{s-k} \otimes e_\ell^{\alpha}$ for $\ell=0,\dots,m-1$:
              \begin{align*}
                  \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k} := \sum_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \D_X[s] (f^{s-k} \otimes e_\ell^{\alpha}) = \bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \D_X[s] (f^{s-k} \otimes e_\ell^{\alpha})
              \end{align*}
              The last equality is also been claimed in this article.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

Now we can identify $s$-action on $\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k}$ now, note $s = -t\partial_t \in \D_Y$, and $\partial_t\in\mathcal{T}_Y$ is a derivation:
\begin{align*}\phantomsection\label{eq:s_action_on_N_m_alpha_k}
    s \cdot (\eta \otimes e_\ell^{\alpha}) & = (s \cdot \eta) \otimes e_\ell^{\alpha} + \eta \otimes (s \cdot e_\ell^{\alpha})     &  & \text{(\cite[Proposition~1.2.9.(i)]{hotta2008d})}  \\
                                           & = (s\eta) \otimes e_\ell^{\alpha}+\eta(-\alpha e^{\alpha}_{\ell}-e^{\alpha}_{\ell-1}) &  & \text{(There is a minus sign in $s=-t\partial_t$)} \\
                                           & =(s-\alpha)\eta \otimes e^{\alpha}_{\ell}-\eta \otimes e^{\alpha}_{\ell-1}
\end{align*}

Since in Sect.~\ref{sect:two_definitions_of_nearby_cycles}, we have
\begin{equation*}
    \begin{array}{ccccccc}
        K_{m-1}^{\alpha} & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & K_m^{\alpha}                              & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & K_{m+1}^{\alpha}                            & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & \cdots \\[1.5ex]
        \overline P      & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & \overline{P \cdot (t\partial_t - \alpha)} & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & \overline{P \cdot (t\partial_t - \alpha)^2} & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & \cdots
    \end{array}
\end{equation*}
Tensor with $R_f[s]f^s$, inducing:
\begin{equation*}\phantomsection\label{construction_of_direct_system_of_N_m_alpha_k}
    \begin{array}{ccccccc}
        R_f[s]f^s \otimes_{\Oy} p_2^* K_{m-1}^{\alpha} & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & R_f[s]f^s \otimes_{\Oy} p_2^* K_m^{\alpha} & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & R_f[s]f^s \otimes_{\Oy} p_2^* K_{m+1}^{\alpha} & \lhook\joinrel\xrightarrow{\quad} & \cdots \\[1.5ex]
        \eta \otimes e^{\alpha}_i                      & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & \eta \otimes e^{\alpha}_i                  & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & \eta \otimes e^{\alpha}_i                      & \xmapsto{\quad}                   & \cdots
    \end{array}
\end{equation*}
So we can restrict to a direct system of $\mathcal{D}_X[s]$-modules:
\[
    \cdots \lhook\joinrel\longrightarrow \mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k} \lhook\joinrel\longrightarrow \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k} \lhook\joinrel\longrightarrow \mathcal{N}_{m+1}^{\alpha,k} \lhook\joinrel\longrightarrow \cdots .
\]

Since by Thm.~\ref{thm:existence_of_b_function}, when we view $\D_X[s]$ module as $\C[s]$ module
through $\C[s]\hookrightarrow \D_X[s]$, then
\[
    \mathrm{Ann}_{\mathbb{C}[s]}\left(\frac{\mathcal{D}_X[s] f^s}{\mathcal{D}_X[s] f^{s+1}}\right)\neq 0
\]
we define $b_f(s)$ to be the monic polynomial of least degree among $b(s)$ such that
\[
    b(s) \cdot \frac{\mathcal{D}_X[s] f^s}{\mathcal{D}_X[s] f^{s+1}} = 0
\]
called the \textbf{b-function} or \textbf{Bernstein-Sato polynomial of} $f$.

\begin{lemma}\label{lemma 10.6}
    Recall the \hyperref[commutative_diagram_of_hyper_surface]{diagram}. In the (derived) category of (quasi-coherent) $\underline{\C}_{X^{\mathrm{an}}}$-modules (so the chain maps here only guarantees isomorphism of cohomology), we have:

    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $\mathrm{DR}\left(\left(R_f[s] f^s \otimes^L_{\mathbb{C}[s]} \mathbb{C}_\alpha\right)^{\mathrm{an}}\right) \simeq \mathrm{DR}\left(\left(R_f[s] f^{s+\alpha} \otimes^L_{\mathbb{C}[s]} \mathbb{C}_0\right)^{\mathrm{an}}\right)\simeq R j_* (f^{-1} L_1^{\lambda^{-1}})$
        \item $\mathrm{DR}\left(\left(\mathcal{D}_X[s] f^{s+k} \otimes^{L}_{\mathbb{C}[s]} \mathbb{C}_\alpha\right)^{\mathrm{an}}\right) \simeq \mathrm{DR}\left(\left(\mathcal{D}_X[s] f^{s+k+\alpha} \otimes^{L}_{\mathbb{C}[s]} \mathbb{C}_0\right)^{\mathrm{an}}\right)\simeq j_! (f^{-1} L_1^{\lambda^{-1}}), \quad k \gg |\alpha|$
    \end{enumerate}
    where $\simeq$ stand for quasi isomorphism, $\mathbb{C}_\alpha := \mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha}\big/\mathfrak{m}_\alpha\mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha}$ is the residue field of $\alpha \in \mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1$, and $\lambda = e^{2\pi i \alpha}$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{remark}
    We defer the explanation of the symbol $R_f[s]f^{s+\alpha}$.
    So this proof doesn't make any claim on the symbol $R_f[s] f^{s+\alpha} \otimes^L_{\mathbb{C}[s]} \mathbb{C}_0$,
    until we \textbf{define} it in the proof of another proposition Prop.~\ref{prop:20.8}.

    Intuitively, however, addition in the exponent corresponds to the multiplication of formal powers,
    and this multiplication will be formalized via a tensor product construction.
\end{remark}
\begin{proof}[Sketch of Proof]

    Take a $\C[s]$-free resolution of $\C_\alpha$:
    \begin{equation*}
        0\longrightarrow \mathbb{C}[s]\xrightarrow{s-\alpha} \mathbb{C}[s]\longrightarrow \frac{\mathbb{C}[s]}{(s-\alpha)}\stackrel{\sim}{\longrightarrow}\frac{\mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha}}{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha\mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha}}=\mathbb{C}_{\alpha}
    \end{equation*}
    then we obtain that
    \begin{align*}
        R_f[s]f^s \otimes^L_{\mathbb{C}[s]} \mathbb{C}_\alpha & \xleftarrow{\substack{\text{quasi-isomorphic}}}\left[ R_f[s]f^s \xrightarrow{s-\alpha} R_f[s]f^s \right] &  & \text{(Tensor with the resolution above)}                                           \\
                                                              & \xrightarrow{\substack{\text{quasi-isomorphic}}} R_f f^\alpha.                                           &  & \text{(see the proof of (4) of Prop.~\ref{prop:properties_of_alpha_nearby_cycles})}
    \end{align*}

    Recall the diagram we have a map $U\xrightarrow{f}\mathbb{A}^{1}_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}$.
    In the proof of (4) of Prop.~\ref{prop:properties_of_alpha_nearby_cycles}, we have shown that
    \[
        R_f \cdot f^\alpha = f^* K_1^\alpha
    \]
    Then we recall Sect.~\ref{sect:two_definitions_of_nearby_cycles}, $K_1^\alpha$, restricted to $\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}$, becomes an integrable connection (a locally free $\mathcal{O}$-module of finite rank).

    \medskip

    The next step is to verify a condition called \textbf{Non-characteristic} \cite[Definition~2.4.2]{hotta2008d}.

    Recall an integrable connection has its characteristic variety is exactly the zero section:
    \[
        \Ch\left(K_1^\alpha\mid_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}}\right) = T^\ast_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}} (\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}) \subset T^\ast (\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\})
    \]
    We have the canonical maps $\varpi$ and $\rho$ (see Lem.~\ref{prop:charvar-closed-emb} for their general construction, which is valid for arbitrary morphisms, not just closed embeddings):
    \begin{align*}
        \varpi: S := U \times_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}} T^\ast (\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}) & \longrightarrow T^\ast (\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}) \\
        \rho: S                                                                                                            & \longrightarrow T^\ast U
    \end{align*}
    Recall $X = \Spec R$ with $U = \Spec R_f$,
    $\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\} = \Spec \mathbb{C}[t, t^{-1}]$.
    The structure morphism is given by
    \begin{align*}
        \phi: \mathbb{C}[t, t^{-1}] & \longrightarrow R_f \\
        t                           & \longmapsto f
    \end{align*}
    Then $T^\ast(\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}) = \Spec \mathbb{C}[t, t^{-1}, \tau]$.
    The zero section $T^\ast_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}}(\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\})$
    is defined by the ideal
    \[
        (\tau) \subset \mathbb{C}[t, t^{-1}, \tau].
    \]
    Similarly, $T^\ast U = \Spec \Sym^\bullet_{R_f}(\mathcal{T}_{R_f})$
    The zero section $T^\ast_U U$ is defined by the ideal of positive degree elements
    \[
        I_U := \Sym_{R_f}^{\ge 1}(\mathcal{T}_{R_f}) \subset \Sym^\bullet_{R_f}(\mathcal{T}_{R_f}).
    \]
    The fiber product $S$ has coordinate ring
    \[
        \mathcal{O}_S = R_f \otimes_{\mathbb{C}[t,t^{-1}]} \mathbb{C}[t,t^{-1},\tau] \simeq R_f[\tau]
    \]
    The map $\varpi$ corresponds to
    \begin{align*}
        \varpi^\#: \mathbb{C}[t,t^{-1},\tau] & \longrightarrow R_f[\tau] \\
        t                                    & \longmapsto f             \\
        \tau                                 & \longmapsto \tau
    \end{align*}
    The map $\rho$ corresponds to
    \begin{align*}
        \rho^\#: \Sym^\bullet_{R_f}(\mathcal{T}_{R_f}) & \longrightarrow R_f[\tau]        \\
        \theta                                         & \longmapsto \tau \cdot \theta(f)
    \end{align*}
    for $\theta \in \Der_\mathbb{C}(R_f)$.

    We then need to calculate the intersection $\varpi^{-1}\left(\Ch(K_1^\alpha\mid_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}})\right) \bigcap \rho^{-1}\left(T^\ast_U U\right)$,
    The core formula is to utilize:
    \[
        V(I)\cap V(J) = V(I \cup J) = V((I \cup J)) = V(I+J)  \quad  \text{ (see \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/00E0}{Tag 00E0}]{stacks-project})}
    \]
    So we:
    \begin{align*}
        I_{\varpi^{-1}\left(\Ch(K_1^\alpha\mid_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}})\right)\bigcap \rho^{-1}\left(T^\ast_U U\right)}
         & = \varpi^\#((\tau))\mathcal{O}_S + \rho^\#(I_U)\mathcal{O}_S         \\
         & = (\tau) + (\tau \cdot \theta(f) \mid \theta \in \Der_\mathbb{C}(A)) \\
         & = (\tau) \subset A[\tau].
    \end{align*}
    Thus, the intersection is exactly the zero space $V((\tau)) \subset \Spec A[\tau]$,
    which is $U \times_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}} T^\ast_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}} (\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\})$.
    We verified that $f$ is non-characteristic with respect to $K_1^\alpha\mid_{\mathbb{A}^1_\C\setminus\{0\}}$.

    \medskip

    By \cite[Proposition~4.7.6]{hotta2008d} (Non-characteristic base change for De Rham complex), we have
    \begin{align*}
        \mathrm{DR}^{\mathrm{Hotta}}_{U}(f^* K_1^\alpha) \simeq f^{-1} \mathrm{DR}^{\mathrm{Hotta}}_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}}(K_1^\alpha)[d_U - d_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}}].
    \end{align*}
    One must be very careful with the convention of the De Rham functor here. In \cite[Sect.~4.7]{hotta2008d},
    the De Rham functor $\mathrm{DR}^{\mathrm{Hotta}}_X(M) := \Omega_X \otimes_{\mathcal{D}_X}^L M$ is concentrated in degrees $[-d_X, 0]$
    (The dualizing sheaf $\Omega_X$ is already at the highest degree).

    However, in our notes Note.~\ref{note 6.2},
    $\mathrm{DR}_{X^{\mathrm{an}}}(M^\mathrm{an})$ is defined as the standard De Rham complex $0 \to M \to M \otimes \Omega^1 \to \dots $ sitting in degrees $[0, d_X]$.
    Therefore,
    \[
        \mathrm{DR}^{\mathrm{Hotta}}_X(\cdot) = \mathrm{DR}_{X^\mathrm{an}}((\cdot)^\mathrm{an})[d_X]
    \]
    Substituting this into the above formula perfectly cancels the dimension shifts:
    \begin{align*}
        \mathrm{DR}_{U^\mathrm{an}}((f^* K_1^\alpha)^\mathrm{an})[d_U] & \simeq f^{-1} \mathrm{DR}_{(\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\})^\mathrm{an}}((K_1^\alpha)^\mathrm{an})[d_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}}] [d_U - d_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}}] \\
                                                                       & \simeq f^{-1} \mathrm{DR}_{(\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\})^\mathrm{an}}((K_1^\alpha)^\mathrm{an})[d_U]
    \end{align*}
    Yielding
    \[
        \mathrm{DR}_{U^\mathrm{an}}((f^* K_1^\alpha)^\mathrm{an}) \simeq f^{-1} \mathrm{DR}_{(\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\})^\mathrm{an}}((K_1^\alpha)^\mathrm{an})
    \]
    exactly, without any residual dimension shift!

    Combining with the fact that its analytic De Rham complex is exactly the local system
    \[
        \mathrm{DR}\left((K_1^\alpha\mid_{\mathbb{A}^1_{\mathbb{C}}\backslash\{0\}})^{\mathrm{an}}\right) = L_1^{\lambda^{-1}},
    \]
    we deduce:
    \begin{align*}
        \mathrm{DR}((R_f \cdot f^\alpha)^{\mathrm{an}})|_{U^{\mathrm{an}}} & \simeq \mathrm{DR}( (f^* K_1^\alpha)^{\mathrm{an}} )  \\
                                                                           & \simeq f^{-1} \mathrm{DR}((K_1^\alpha)^{\mathrm{an}}) \\
                                                                           & \simeq f^{-1} L_1^{\lambda^{-1}}.
    \end{align*}

                  \DR_{X^\mathrm{an}}\Bigl(\bigl(\mathcal D_X[s]\cdot f^{s+k}\ \otimes^L_{\C[s]}\ \C_\alpha \bigr)^{\an}\Bigr)

\begin{lemma} \label{lemma 10.7}
    $$
        (b_f(s-k+\alpha))^m \quad\mathrm{kills}\quad \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k}\big/\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k-1}
    $$
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    By \hyperref[construction_of_direct_system_of_N_m_alpha_k]{construction}, $\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k}$ is generated by $f^{s-k} \otimes e_0^\alpha, \dots, f^{s-k} \otimes e_{m-1}^\alpha$.
    We can identify $\mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k}$ with the submodule generated by the first $m-1$ elements.
    This yields a short exact sequence of $\mathcal{D}_X[s]$-modules:
    $$
        0\longrightarrow \mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k} \longrightarrow \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k} \longrightarrow Q \longrightarrow 0
    $$
    where the quotient module $Q := \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k} \big/ \mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k}$
    is a cyclic $\mathcal{D}_X[s]$-module generated by the image of $f^{s-k} \otimes e_{m-1}^\alpha$,
    which we denote by $f^{s-k} \otimes \overline{e}_{m-1}^{\alpha}$.

    Recall the $s$-action on the basis elements gives:
    $$
        s \cdot (f^{s-k} \otimes e^{\alpha}_{m-1}) = (s-\alpha) (f^{s-k} \otimes e^{\alpha}_{m-1}) - f^{s-k} \otimes e^{\alpha}_{m-2}
    $$
    In the quotient $Q$, the term belonging to $\mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k}$ vanishes, yielding
    $$
        s \cdot (f^{s-k} \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1})=(s-\alpha)(f^{s-k} \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1})
    $$
    We want to show that $(b_f(s-k-\alpha))^m$ kills $\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k} \big/ \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k-1}$.
    Taking the above exact sequence modulo the submodule $\mathcal{N}_{m}^{\alpha, k-1}$, we get the exact sequence:
    $$
        0\longrightarrow \frac{\mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k}}{\mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k} \cap \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k-1}} \longrightarrow \frac{\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k}}{\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k-1}} \longrightarrow \overline{Q} \longrightarrow 0
    $$
    where $\overline{Q} = Q \big/ (Q \cap N_m^{\alpha,k-1})$ is a cyclic $\mathcal{D}_X[s]$-module generated by the image of $f^{s-k} \otimes \overline{e}_{m-1}^{\alpha}$.

    Rather than identifying $\overline{Q}$ with a quotient of $\mathcal{D}_X[s]$, we can calculate the action of $b_f(s-k+\alpha)$ directly.
    Recall we have:
    $$
        s \cdot (f^{s-k} \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1}) = (s f^{s-k}) \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1} - \alpha (f^{s-k} \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1}) = ((s-\alpha) f^{s-k}) \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1}
    $$
    But what about the action of $\mathcal{D}_X \subset \mathcal{D}_X[s]$?

    Recall the Step 2 of Def.~\ref{def:N_m_alpha_k}, we have:
    \[
        \theta(\psi \otimes s) = \theta(\psi) \otimes s + \psi \cdot \theta(t) \otimes \partial_t s
    \]
    Where $\theta \in \mathcal{T}_Y\subset\D_Y$,
    section $\psi \in \mathcal{O}_Y = R[t]$, and $s \in K_m^{-\alpha}$, using the fact that $t$ is the coordinate of $\mathbb{A}^1_\C$ and $p_2^*(t) = t$.

    Given $D\in\D_X$, since any vector field $\theta \in \mathcal{T}_X\subseteq\mathcal{T}_Y$ has $\theta(t)=0$, so:
    $$
        D \cdot (\eta \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1}) = (D \cdot \eta) \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1}.
    $$
    So for any polynomial $P(s) \in \mathcal{D}_X[s]$ with coefficients in $\mathcal{D}_X$, we have:
    $$
        P(s) \cdot (f^{s-k} \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1}) = (P(s-\alpha) f^{s-k}) \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1}
    $$
    We want to calculate the action of $b_f(s-k+\alpha)$.
    Since by Thm~.\ref{thm:existence_of_b_function}, we have $\widetilde{P}(s) \in \mathcal{D}_X[s]$ such that
    $$
        b_f(s) f^s = \widetilde{P}(s) f^{s+1} \in R_f[s]f^s
    $$
    Shifting the variable $s \mapsto s-k$, we get $b_f(s-k)f^{s-k} = \widetilde{P}(s-k)f^{s-k+1}$.
    Using this relation, we calculate:
    \begin{align*}
        b_f(s-k+\alpha) \cdot (f^{s-k} \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1})
         & = (b_f((s-\alpha)-k+\alpha) f^{s-k}) \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1}          \\
         & = (b_f(s-k) f^{s-k}) \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1}                          \\
         & = (\widetilde{P}(s-k) f^{s-k+1}) \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1}              \\
         & = \widetilde{P}(s-k+\alpha) \cdot (f^{s-k+1} \otimes \overline{e}^\alpha_{m-1})
    \end{align*}
    Thus $b_f(s-k+\alpha)$ exactly kills $\overline{Q}$.

    Now we proceed by induction on $m$.
    For the base case $m=1$, the submodule $\mathcal{N}_0^{\alpha,k}$ is 0 by definition.
    Therefore $Q \simeq \mathcal{N}_1^{\alpha,k}$, and the quotient $\overline{Q}$ is exactly $\mathcal{N}_1^{\alpha,k} \big/ \mathcal{N}_1^{\alpha,k-1}$.
    Since we just showed $b_f(s-k+\alpha)$ kills $\overline{Q}$, the base case holds.

    For the induction step, we assume $(b_f(s-k+\alpha))^{m-1}$ kills $\mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k} \big/ \mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k-1}$. Recall the exact sequence:
    $$
        0\longrightarrow \frac{\mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k}}{\mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k} \cap \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k-1}} \longrightarrow \frac{\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k}}{\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k-1}} \longrightarrow \overline{Q} \longrightarrow 0
    $$
    Since the left term in the sequence is a quotient of $\mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k} \big/ \mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k-1}$ (as $\mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k-1} \subset \mathcal{N}_{m-1}^{\alpha,k} \cap \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k-1}$), $(b_f(s-k+\alpha))^{m-1}$ kills the left term as well.
    Since $b_{f}(s-k+\alpha)$ kills the right term $\overline{Q}$, their product $(b_f(s-k+\alpha))^{m}$ kills the middle term $\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k} \big/ \mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k-1}$, completing the proof.

\end{proof}

    And the definiion in \cite{hotta2008d} is also a proposition, it claims such defined De Rham functor preserve quasi-isomorphisms.
    So the next step is first to manipulate in derived category, before applying the De Rham functor.

    \medskip

    Let $\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0$ be the maximal ideal of $0 \in \mathbb{A}_{\mathbb{C}}^1$. We first consider the base change property:
    \begin{align*}
        \left[\mathcal{N}_{m}^{-\alpha,k} \xrightarrow{s} \mathcal{N}_{m}^{-\alpha,k}\right] & \stackrel{\text{q.i}}{\simeq} \mathcal{N}^{-\alpha,k} \otimes^{L}_{\mathbb{C}[s]} \mathbb{C}_{0}                                                                            \\
                                                                                             & \stackrel{\text{q.i}}{\simeq} \left[\mathcal{N}^{-\alpha,k}_{m,\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_{0}} \xrightarrow{s}\mathcal{N}^{-\alpha,k}_{m,\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_{0}}\right] \\
                                                                                             & \text{(See Step 5 of Rmk.~\ref{rmk:continue_prove_lemma_10.6}, taking localization has no effect on (derived) tensor with $\C_\alpha$)}
    \end{align*}

    Next, we analyze the localization of $\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,k}$ at $\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0=(s)$.
    By Lemma \ref{lemma 10.7}, replacing $\alpha$ with $-\alpha$, we know that the operator $(b_f(s-j-\alpha))^m$ kills the quotient $\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j}\big/\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j-1}$.
    Since $b_f(s)$ is a non-zero polynomial, its roots are finite. Thus, for $j \gg 0$, $-j-\alpha$ is not a root of $b_f(s)$, which implies:
    \[
        (b_f(-j-\alpha))^m \neq 0
    \]
    When we localize at the maximal ideal $\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0 = (s)$ (i.e., evaluating at $s=0$), the annihilator $(b_f(s-j-\alpha))^m$ becomes an invertible unit in the localized ring $\mathbb{C}[s]_{\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0}$ for all $j \gg 0$.
    Because the quotient module $\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j}\big/\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j-1}$ is killed by a unit upon localization, the localized quotient must be zero:
    \[
        \left(\frac{\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j}}{\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j-1}}\right)_{\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0} = 0, \quad \text{for } j \gg 0.
    \]
    This implies an isomorphism of the localized modules for sufficiently large indices:
    \[
        (\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j})_{\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0} \simeq (\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j-1})_{\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0}, \quad \text{for } j \gg 0.
    \]
    Therefore, for any fixed $k \gg 0$, the localized module stabilizes as we take the sequential limit going upward to infinity (tensor product commutes with direct limits):
    \[
        (\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,k})_{\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0} \simeq \varinjlim_{j} (\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j})_{\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0} \simeq \left( \varinjlim_j \mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j} \right)_{\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0}.
    \]
    Note this is NOT the \hyperref[construction_of_direct_system_of_N_m_alpha_k]{direct system} for $\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,k}$ mentioned before.

    By construction, the direct limit $\varinjlim_j \mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j}$ corresponds to adjoining all negative powers of $f$, which precisely generates the full algebraic localization $R_f[s]f^s$ along with the bases $e_\ell^{-\alpha}$. That is:
    \[
        \varinjlim_{j} \mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,j} = \bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \big(R_f[s]f^s\big) \otimes e_\ell^{-\alpha}.
    \]
    For one wonder why RHS is a $\D_X[s]$ submodule, see the proof of Lem.~\ref{lemma 10.7} for the explanation of $\D_X[s]$ action.

    Then do the reverse arguments at the begging of proof, which claims that adding localization doesn't change quasi-isomorphism.
    We can drop the localization here.
    \begin{align*}
        \left[\mathcal{N}^{-\alpha,k}_{m,\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_{0}} \xrightarrow{s}\mathcal{N}^{-\alpha,k}_{m,\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_{0}}\right] & \simeq \left[\left(\bigoplus \limits_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha}\right)_{\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0} \xrightarrow{s} \left(\bigoplus \limits_{\ell=0}^{m-1}\left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha}\right)_{\underline{\mathfrak{m}}_0}\right] \\
                                                                                                                                                      & \stackrel{\text{q.i}}{\simeq} \left[\bigoplus \limits_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha} \xrightarrow{s} \bigoplus \limits_{\ell=0}^{m-1}\left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha}\right]
    \end{align*}

    The original lecture note says, one can prove (1) by induction on $m$.
    But instead of doing that, we will prove it directly,
    using the black box introduced in Step 3 of Rmk.~\ref{rmk:continue_prove_lemma_10.6}.

    At this point we think
    \[
        C_m^\bullet := \left[\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha, k} \xrightarrow{s} \mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha, k}\right] \stackrel{\mathrm{q.i.}}{\simeq} \left[\bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha} \xrightarrow{s} \bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha}\right]
    \]
    is a complex of $\D_X$(without $s$)-modules, and $\DR$ should be taken over $X$.

    We localize at $U\subseteq X$, we discover there is no change in representation:
    \[
        C_m^\bullet|_{U} \stackrel{\mathrm{q.i.}}{\simeq} \left[\bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha} \xrightarrow{s} \bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha}\right]
    \]
    Multiplication by $s$ induce an injection, so we only need to compute the cokernel.

    We claim that
    \[
        \mathrm{Coker}(s) \simeq \bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(R_{f} \cdot f^{0}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha}
    \]
    Which is a \textbf{free} $R_f$-module of rank $m$.

    We equip \textbf{two} (left) $R_f[s]$-modules structures on $\bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha}$.
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item The \textbf{first} one induced by identification $s\mapsto -t\partial_t$ (still different with the previous section Sect.~\ref{sect:Bjork_s_approach_to_existence_of_b-functions}'s $-\partial_t {t}$).

              This structure is used to define the differential $s_M := s$ in $C_m^\bullet$.

        \item The \textbf{second} one is just to multiply on the first factor of $\left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha}$.

              This structure is used to calculate the cokernel of $s_M$.

              And under the second structure, $s_M$ is $R_f[s]$-linear, recall the \hyperref[eq:s_action_on_N_m_alpha_k]{$s$-action formula}:
              \begin{align*}
                  s_M(a s^k \cdot u_\ell) & = s_M\big(as^k f^s \otimes e_\ell^{-\alpha}\big)                                                  \\
                                          & = s_M\big(as^k f^s\big) \otimes e_\ell^{-\alpha} + as^k f^s \otimes s_M\big(e_\ell^{-\alpha}\big) \\
                                          & =(s - \alpha)as^k f^s \otimes e_\ell^{-\alpha} - as^k f^s \otimes e_{\ell-1}^{-\alpha}            \\
                                          & = as^k\big((s - \alpha)f^s \otimes e_\ell^{-\alpha} - f^s \otimes e_{\ell-1}^{-\alpha}\big)       \\
                                          & = as^k \cdot s_M(u_\ell).
              \end{align*}
    \end{enumerate}
    Using the second structure, we identify
    \[
        \bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha} \simeq \bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} R_{f}[s]
    \]
    We denote the standard basis (the second $s$ structure) of RHS as $u_\ell := f^s \otimes e^{-\alpha}_\ell$ for $\ell = 0, 1, \dots, m-1$.

    Then $s_M$ corresponds to the following map:
    \[
        s_M(u_\ell) = (s+\alpha)u_\ell - u_{\ell-1}
    \]
    for $\ell = 0, 1, \dots, m-1$, where $u_{-1} := 0$.

    Taking quotient to $M:=\bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(R_{f}[s]f^{s}\right) \to \mathrm{Coker}(sR_f[s]\big/(s+\alpha)_M)$, we have $(s+\alpha)\overline u_0=0$ and recursively
    \[
        \overline u_{\ell-1}=(s+\alpha)\overline u_\ell\quad\Rightarrow\quad
        \overline u_{m-1-k}=(s+\alpha)^k\overline u_{m-1}\qquad (k=0,\dots,m-1).
    \]
    Therefore $\mathrm{Coker}(s_M)$ is generated by $\overline u_{m-1}$ as an $R_f[s]$-module, and
    \[
        (s+\alpha)^m\overline u_{m-1}=\overline u_{-1}=0.
    \]
    Thus the $R_f[s]$-linear (we use the second $R_f[s]$ structure on) map
    \[
        \Phi:R_f[s]\longrightarrow \mathrm{Coker}(s_M),\qquad a\longmapsto a\overline u_{m-1}
    \]
    factors through a surjection
    \[
        \widetilde\Phi:R_f[s]\big/(s+\alpha)^m \twoheadrightarrow \mathrm{Coker}(s_M).
    \]
    Conversely, define an $R_f[s]$-linear map
    \[
        \Psi:M\longrightarrow R_f[s]\big/(s+\alpha)^m,\qquad
        \Psi(u_\ell)=(s+\alpha)^{m-1-\ell}\bmod (s+\alpha)^m.
    \]
    Then:
    \[
        \Psi\bigl(s_M(u_\ell)\bigr)
        =\Psi\bigl((s+\alpha)u_\ell-u_{\ell-1}\bigr)
        =(s+\alpha)^{m-\ell}-(s+\alpha)^{m-\ell}=0,
    \]
    Hence $\Psi$ vanishes on $s_M(M)$ and factors through
    \[
        \widetilde\Psi:\mathrm{Coker}(s_M)\longrightarrow R_f[s]\big/(s+\alpha)^m.
    \]
    One can check $\widetilde\Phi$ and $\widetilde\Psi$ are inverse to each other. That is
    \[
        \widetilde\Psi\circ \widetilde\Phi=\mathrm{id}_{R_f[s]\big/(s+\alpha)^m} \quad \mathrm{\textbf{and}} \quad \widetilde\Phi\circ \widetilde\Psi=\mathrm{id}_{\mathrm{Coker}(s_M)}.
    \]
    Finally, since $(s+\alpha)^m$ is a monic polynomial of degree $m$ in the variable $s$,
    every class in $R_f[s]\big/(s+\alpha)^m = R_f[s+\alpha]\big/(s+\alpha)^m$ has a unique representative of the form
    $\sum_{k=0}^{m-1} a_k (s+\alpha)^k$ with $a_k\in R_f$.
    Thus
    \[
        1,\, (s+\alpha),\, \dots,\, (s+\alpha)^{m-1}
    \]
    is an $R_f$-basis,
    and transporting it via $\widetilde\Phi$ gives the claimed $R_f$-basis
    \[
        \overline u_{m-1},\, (s+\alpha)\overline u_{m-1},\, \dots,\, (s+\alpha)^{m-1}\overline u_{m-1}.
    \]
    And we note again, multiplying by $s$ here is of \textbf{the second} $R_f[s]$-module structure.

    For any vector field $\theta \in \mathcal{T}_X \subset \mathcal{T}_Y$, since $\theta(t) = 0$,
    recall Step 6 of Ex.~\ref{example:direct_image_of_graph_embedding} and proof in Lem.~\ref{lemma 10.7},
    its action on $u_\ell = f^s \otimes e^{-\alpha}_\ell \in M$ under the first module structure is:
    \begin{align*}
         & \theta(\psi \otimes \eta) = \theta(\psi) \otimes \eta + \psi \cdot \theta(t) \otimes \partial_t \eta                                              \\
         & \implies \theta \cdot u_\ell = \theta(f^s) \otimes e^{-\alpha}_\ell = s f^{s-1} \theta(f) \otimes e^{-\alpha}_\ell = \frac{\theta(f)}{f} s u_\ell
    \end{align*}
    where the $s$ multiplication on the right is exactly the $s$ acting through \textbf{the second} $R_f[s]$-module structure.

    Thus, on the quotient $\mathrm{Coker}(s_M)$, the vector field $\theta$ acts on the basis elements $v_k := (s+\alpha)^k \overline u_{m-1}$ for $k = 0, \dots, m-1$ as follows:
    \begin{align*}
        \theta \cdot v_k & = \theta \cdot \big( (s+\alpha)^k \overline u_{m-1} \big)                                                                                                                           \\
                         & = (s+\alpha)^k (\theta \cdot \overline u_{m-1})                                                              &  & \text{(all are in \textbf{the second} $R_f[s]$-module structure)} \\
                         & = (s+\alpha)^k \left( \frac{\theta(f)}{f} s \overline u_{m-1} \right)                                                                                                               \\
                         & = \frac{\theta(f)}{f} (s+\alpha)^k \big( (s+\alpha) \overline u_{m-1} - \alpha \overline u_{m-1} \big)                                                                              \\
                         & = \frac{\theta(f)}{f} \big( (s+\alpha)^{k+1} \overline u_{m-1} - \alpha (s+\alpha)^k \overline u_{m-1} \big)                                                                        \\
                         & = \frac{\theta(f)}{f} (v_{k+1} - \alpha v_k)
    \end{align*}
    where we identify $v_m = (s+\alpha)^m \overline u_{m-1} = 0$.

    On the other hand,
    \[
        f^*\big( K^\alpha_m\mid_{\mathbb{A}^1_\C \setminus \{0\}} \big) = R_f \otimes_{\C[t,t^{-1}]}\bigoplus_{i=0}^{m-1} \mathbb{C}[t, t^{-1}] e^\alpha_i
    \]
    is also a rank $m$ $R_f$-module. And we calculate the $\D_U$-module structure on it by \cite[Sect.~1.3]{hotta2008d}:
    for any vector field $\theta \in \mathcal{T}_U$, function $\psi \in R_f$, and section $s \in K^\alpha_m\mid_{\mathbb{A}^1_\C \setminus \{0\}}$, the inverse image connection is given by
    \[
        \theta(\psi \otimes s) = \theta(\psi) \otimes s + \psi \, \theta(t \circ f) \otimes \partial_t s
    \]
    Taking the canonical generic basis $s = e^\alpha_i$, we recall the connection on $K^\alpha_m$ is determined by $t\partial_t e^\alpha_i = \alpha e^\alpha_i + e^\alpha_{i-1}$ (where $e^\alpha_{-1} = 0$).
    This implies $\partial_t e^\alpha_i = \frac{1}{t}(\alpha e^\alpha_i + e^\alpha_{i-1})$.
    Applying the formula to the basis $1 \otimes e^\alpha_i$ of the pullback, and using the fact that the coordinate $t$ acts as $f$ on the pullback $f^* K^\alpha_m$:
    \begin{align*}
        \theta \cdot (1 \otimes e^\alpha_i) & = \theta(1) \otimes e^\alpha_i + 1 \cdot \theta(f) \otimes \partial_t e^\alpha_i             \\
                                            & = \theta(f) \otimes \frac{1}{t}(\alpha e^\alpha_i + e^\alpha_{i-1})                          \\
                                            & = \frac{\theta(f)}{f} \otimes (\alpha e^\alpha_i + e^\alpha_{i-1})                           \\
                                            & = \frac{\theta(f)}{f} \big( \alpha (1 \otimes e^\alpha_i) + (1 \otimes e^\alpha_{i-1}) \big)
    \end{align*}
    Replacing $\alpha$ with $-\alpha$ to strictly match the parameter of the system we investigated, the pullback
    $f^* K^{-\alpha}_m$ carries exactly the action
    \[
        \theta \cdot (1 \otimes e^{-\alpha}_i) = \frac{\theta(f)}{f} \big( -\alpha(1 \otimes e^{-\alpha}_i) + (1 \otimes e^{-\alpha}_{i-1}) \big)
    \]
    By matching our arbitrary basis elements $v_k \leftrightarrow 1 \otimes e^{-\alpha}_{m-1-k}$, we recover exactly the same matrix operator we derived strictly from algebraic quotients:
    \[
        \theta \cdot v_k = \frac{\theta(f)}{f} (v_{k+1} - \alpha v_k)
    \]
    This confirms that the module $\mathrm{Coker}(s_M)$ correctly recovers the restriction $f^* K^{-\alpha}_m$.

    By the same ($f$ is) non-characteristic (with respect to $K^\alpha_m\mid_{\mathbb{A}^1_\C \setminus \{0\}}$) arguments presented in proof in Lem.~\ref{lemma 10.6}, we get:
    \[
        \mathrm{DR}_{U^\mathrm{an}} \big( (\mathrm{Coker}(s_M)\mid_U)^{\mathrm{an}} \big) \simeq f^{-1} L_m^{\lambda}.
    \]

    To conclude over the entire $X$, we (again) apply the Stabilization of Log De Rham for Lattices (see Step 3 and 4 in Rmk.~\ref{rmk:continue_prove_lemma_10.6}):
    \[
        \DR_{X^\mathrm{an}} \left(\left[\mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,k} \xrightarrow{s} \mathcal{N}_m^{-\alpha,k}\right]^\mathrm{an}\right) \simeq \DR_{X^\mathrm{an}} \left(\mathrm{Coker}(s_M)^\mathrm{an}\right) \simeq Rj_*\mathrm{DR}_{U^\mathrm{an}} \big( (\mathrm{Coker}(s_M)\mid_U)^{\mathrm{an}} \big)  \simeq Rj_*\big(f^{-1} L_m^\lambda\big)
    \]
    which establishes (1).

    \bigskip

    \noindent\textbf{For proving (2)}, assume $k\gg 0$, since we can't find a free $R_f$-module structure for :
    \[
        \mathrm{Coker}\left(\bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(\D_X[s]f^{s+k}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha} \xrightarrow{s} \bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left(\D_X[s]f^{s+k}\right) \otimes e_{\ell}^{-\alpha}\right)
    \]
    we use the five lemma (on $U$) by doing induction on $m$.

    Fix $k\ge k_0$ and set
    \[
        C_m^{+}:=\Big[\ \mathcal N_m^{-\alpha,k}\xrightarrow{s}\mathcal N_m^{-\alpha,k}\ \Big],\qquad
        C_m^{-}:=\Big[\ \mathcal N_m^{-\alpha,-k}\xrightarrow{s}\mathcal N_m^{-\alpha,-k}\ \Big],
    \]
    viewed as two--term complexes in degrees $-1, 0$.

    \medskip

    \noindent\textbf{Step 1 (assume the $Rj_*$ part is done).}
    Assume Proposition~22.11(1) has been proved, i.e.
    \[
        \mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{+})\simeq Rj_*\bigl(f^{-1}L_m^\lambda\bigr)\qquad(\forall m).
    \]
    Restricting to $U$ gives:
    \[
        j^{-1}\mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{+})
        \simeq j^{-1}Rj_*\bigl(f^{-1}L_m^\lambda\bigr)
        \simeq f^{-1}L_m^\lambda,
    \]
    so $j^{-1}\mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{+})$ is a local system sitting in degree $0$.

    Why $j^{-1}Rj_* \simeq \mathrm{id}$?
    First, by \cite[Eq.~C.2.6]{hotta2008d} $j^{-1}$ is exact, so $j^{-1} = Rj^{-1}$.
    Then by \cite[Proposition B.5.7.]{hotta2008d} and $j^{-1}$ is exact, we get $Rj^{-1}\circ Rj_* = R(j^{-1}\circ j_*)$.
    Finally apply the counit isomorphism (since $j$ is an open immersion) of the adjunction pair in \cite[Eq.~C.1.10]{hotta2008d}, we get $R(j^{-1}\circ j_*) = R(\mathrm{id}) = \mathrm{id}$.


    \medskip
    \noindent\textbf{Step 2 (exact sequences in $m$ and the map $C_m^{-}\to C_m^{+}$).}
    The rank--$m$ connection $K_m^{-\alpha}$ on $\mathbb{A}^1\setminus\{0\}$ has the filtration of $\D_{\mathbb{A}^1\setminus\{0\}}$-module
    \[
        0\subset \C[t,t^{-1}] e^{-\alpha}_0\subset\cdots\subset\bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-2}\C[t,t^{-1}]  e^{-\alpha}_\ell \subset K_m^{-\alpha}
    \]
    with successive quotient $K_1^{-\alpha}$.
    By functoriality of the construction of $\mathcal N_m^{-\alpha,\pm k}$ from the generators
    $f^{s\mp k}\otimes e_\ell$, this gives short exact sequences of $\mathcal D_X[s]$--modules
    \[
        0\to \mathcal N_{m-1}^{-\alpha,\pm k}\longrightarrow \mathcal N_m^{-\alpha,\pm k}
        \longrightarrow \mathcal N_1^{-\alpha,\pm k}\to 0,
    \]
    hence short exact sequences of complexes
    \[
        0\to C_{m-1}^{\pm}\longrightarrow C_m^{\pm}\longrightarrow C_1^{\pm}\to 0.
        \tag{$\ast_\pm$}
    \]
    Moreover $f^{s+k}=f^{2k}f^{s-k}$ implies $\mathcal N_m^{-\alpha,-k}\subset \mathcal N_m^{-\alpha,k}$,
    so we have a morphism of complexes $C_m^{-}\to C_m^{+}$ compatible with $(\ast_\pm)$.

    \medskip
    \noindent\textbf{Step 3 (restrict to $U$ and do the induction using the short five lemma).}
    By \cite[Sect.~4.2]{hotta2008d}, $\DR$ has finitely many non-vanishing cohomology sheaves, define objects on $U$:
    \[
        K_m^{-}:=j^{-1}\mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{-})\in D^b(\underline{\C}_{U^{\mathrm{an}}}),\qquad
        K_m^{+}:=j^{-1}\mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{+})\simeq f^{-1}L_m^\lambda.
    \]
    For $m=1$, Lemma~\ref{lemma 10.6} (2) gives $\mathrm{DR}_X(C_1^{-})\simeq j_!(f^{-1}L_1^\lambda)$, hence by
    \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/00A7}{Tag 00A7}]{stacks-project} and equivalence between \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/00A4}{Tag 00A4}]{stacks-project} and \cite[Definition~C.1.1]{hotta2008d}, we get:
    \[
        K_1^{-}=j^{-1}\mathrm{DR}_X(C_1^{-})\simeq f^{-1}L_1^\lambda
    \]
    (concentrated in degree $0$).

    \medskip

    We recall a fact in derived category (of a homotopy category), all exact sequences of complexes are distinguished triangles.

    To see this, using \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/08RI}{Tag 08RI}]{stacks-project}, we get $\varphi$ to make the diagram below commute:
    \[
        \begin{tikzcd}
            \quad       & 0  \arrow[r]                   & L^\bullet \arrow[r] \arrow[d, equal] & \mathrm{Cone}(K^\bullet\to L^\bullet) \arrow[r] \arrow[d, "\varphi"] & K^\bullet[1] \arrow[r] & 0 \\
            0 \arrow[r] & K^\bullet \arrow[r]                  & L^\bullet \arrow[r]                  & M^\bullet \arrow[r]                                                      &  0
        \end{tikzcd}
    \]
    Since short exact sequence of complexes lead to long exact sequences of cohomology, we get commutative diagram:
    \[
        \begin{tikzcd}
            \cdots \arrow[r] & H^n(L^\bullet) \arrow[r] \arrow[d, equal] & H^n(\mathrm{Cone}(K^\bullet\to L^\bullet)) \arrow[r] \arrow[d, "H^n(\varphi)"] & H^{n+1}(K^\bullet) \arrow[r] \arrow[d, equal] & H^{n+1}(L^\bullet) \arrow[r] \arrow[d, equal] & \cdots \\
            \cdots \arrow[r] & H^n(L^\bullet) \arrow[r]                  & H^n(M^\bullet) \arrow[r, "\delta"]                                             & H^{n+1}(K^\bullet) \arrow[r]                  & H^{n+1}(L^\bullet) \arrow[r]                  & \cdots
        \end{tikzcd}
    \]
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item  by \cite[Lemma~1.5.3]{Weibel1994HA}, the right square is commutative.
        \item  by \cite[Exercise.~1.5.6]{Weibel1994HA}, the middle square is commutative.
    \end{enumerate}
    and by five-lemma, we get $\varphi$ is a quasi-isomorphism.

    Then by \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/014L}{Tag 014L} and \href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/014Q}{Tag 014Q}]{stacks-project} the triangle
    \[
        K^\bullet \to L^\bullet \to \mathrm{Cone}(K^\bullet\to L^\bullet) \to K^\bullet[1]
    \]
    is a distinguished triangle in homotopy category.

    So we get a morphism between triangles in derived category:
    \[
        \begin{tikzcd}
            K^\bullet \arrow[r] \arrow[d, equal] & L^\bullet \arrow[r] \arrow[d, equal] & \mathrm{Cone}(K^\bullet\to L^\bullet) \arrow[r] \arrow[d, "\text{q.i.}"] &[4em] K^\bullet[1] \arrow[d, equal] \\
            K^\bullet \arrow[r]                  & L^\bullet \arrow[r]                  & M^\bullet \arrow[r,"\text{NOT a chain map}"]                                  & K^\bullet[1]
        \end{tikzcd}
    \]
    So
    \[
        K^\bullet \to L^\bullet \to M^\bullet \xrightarrow{\text{Not a chain map}} K^\bullet[1]
    \]
    is a distinguished triangle in the derived one \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/05RU}{Tag 05RU}, \href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/05R6}{Tag 05R6} and TR1 of \href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/0145}{Tag 0145}]{stacks-project}.

    \medskip

    Induction hypothesis: assume $K_{m-1}^{-}\simeq f^{-1}L_{m-1}^\lambda$ (degree $0$).
    Apply $j^{-1}\mathrm{DR}_X$ to $(\ast_-)$ to get a distinguished triangle
    (apply \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/014X}{Tag 0G6E}]{stacks-project} to
    $\Omega_{X^{\mathrm{an}}} \otimes_{\D_{X^{\mathrm{an}}}} -$,
    with \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/05S8}{Tag 05S8} and \href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/05SC}{Tag 05SC}]{stacks-project},
    we get $\DR_X$ is an exact functor \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/014V}{Tag 014V}]{stacks-project})
    \[
        K_{m-1}^{-}\to K_m^{-}\to K_1^{-}\to \cdots.
    \]
    Since $K_{m-1}^{-}$ and $K_1^{-}$ are both concentrated in degree $0$,
    the long exact sequence of cohomology sheaves \cite[TR2 in \href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/0145}{Tag 0145}, \href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/05QL}{Tag 05QL}, \href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/0147}{Tag 0147}, \href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/0148}{Tag 0148}, \href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/05RS}{Tag 05RS} and \href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/05RT}{Tag 05RT}]{stacks-project} implies:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item $K_m^{-}$ is also concentrated in degree $0$, and
        \item we obtain a short exact sequence of local systems on $U$,
              \[
                  0\to f^{-1}L_{m-1}^\lambda \longrightarrow K_m^{-}\longrightarrow f^{-1}L_1^\lambda\to 0.
                  \tag{$\dagger$}
              \]
    \end{enumerate}

    On the other hand, by Step 1, the Jordan local systems of the maximal extension $C^+_m$ have the canonical short exact sequence
    \[
        0\to f^{-1}L_{m-1}^\lambda \longrightarrow f^{-1}L_m^\lambda\longrightarrow f^{-1}L_1^\lambda\to 0.
        \tag{$\ddagger$}
    \]
    The morphism of complexes $C_\bullet^{-}\to C_\bullet^{+}$ induces a morphism
    of exact sequences $(\dagger)\to(\ddagger)$:
    \[
        \begin{CD}
            0 @>>> f^{-1}L_{m-1}^\lambda @>>> K_m^{-} @>>> f^{-1}L_1^\lambda @>>> 0\\
            @. @VV{\simeq}V @VV{b_m}V @VV{\simeq}V @.\\
            0 @>>> f^{-1}L_{m-1}^\lambda @>>> f^{-1}L_m^\lambda @>>> f^{-1}L_1^\lambda @>>> 0 .
        \end{CD}
    \]
    The left and right vertical maps are isomorphisms by induction and the base case $m=1$.
    Hence, by the short five lemma, $b_m$ is an isomorphism, i.e.
    \[
        j^{-1}\mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{-})\simeq f^{-1}L_m^\lambda.
        \tag{$\clubsuit$}
    \]

    \medskip
    \noindent\textbf{Step 4 (show $\mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{-})$ is supported on $U$).}
    For $m=1$, $\mathrm{DR}_X(C_1^{-})\simeq j_!(f^{-1}L_1^\lambda)$ is supported on $U$.
    Assuming $\mathrm{DR}_X(C_{m-1}^{-})$ is supported on $U$, apply $i^{-1}$ ($i:D = X\setminus U\hookrightarrow X$)
    to the triangle coming from $(\ast_-)$ after $\mathrm{DR}_X$:
    \[
        \mathrm{DR}_X(C_{m-1}^{-})\to \mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{-})\to \mathrm{DR}_X(C_1^{-})\to \cdots.
    \]
    Since $i^{-1}$ is an exact functor (between derived categories) and kills the (cohomology of) two end terms,
    it also kills the (cohomology of) middle term;
    hence $\mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{-})$ has cohomology supported on $U$.

    \medskip
    \noindent\textbf{Step 5 (reverse Step 3: extend back to $X$).}
    For an open immersion $j$, any complex $K = K^\bullet$ with cohomology supported on $U$
    satisfies $K\simeq j_!j^{-1}K$
    (since $j_!$ is exact by \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/01AK}{Tag 01AK}]{stacks-project}, we have $j_! = Rj_!$; moreover, \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/00A8}{Tag 00A8}]{stacks-project} establishes this isomorphism when the complex $K$ is concentrated in a single degree).

    Applying this to $K=\mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{-})$ and using $(\clubsuit)$ yields
    \[
        \mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{-})
        \simeq j_!j^{-1}\mathrm{DR}_X(C_m^{-})
        \simeq j_!(f^{-1}L_m^\lambda).
    \]
    This proves (2) for $m$, completing the induction.

\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:the_linear_operator_in_colimit}
    Let $W$ be a $\mathbb{C}$-vector space, and let $\varphi$ be a $\mathbb{C}$-linear operator on $W$ admitting a minimal polynomial.
    Set $W_{\infty} = \bigoplus_{k=0}^{\infty} W \otimes e_k$ and define $$\varphi_{\infty}(w \otimes e_i) = (\varphi - \alpha)w \otimes e_i - w \otimes e_{i-1} \quad \text{for all }  w \in W, \; (e_{-1} = 0).$$Then $\varphi_{\infty}$
    is surjective and $\ker(\varphi_{\infty}) \simeq W_{\alpha}$, where $W_{\alpha}$ is the generalized $\alpha$-eigenspace of $\varphi$ in $W$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    Define the linear map:
    \begin{equation}
        \begin{aligned}\label{eq:alpha_eigenspace_map}
            W_{\alpha} & \longrightarrow \ker(\varphi_{\infty})                                       \\
            w          & \longmapsto \sum\limits_{i \geqslant 0} (\varphi - \alpha)^i w \otimes e_{i}
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation}
    Let us verify that this map is indeed an isomorphism:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Well-defined (maps to kernel):} For $w \in W_\alpha$, the operator $\varphi-\alpha$ is nilpotent, so the sum is finite. Evaluating $\varphi_\infty$ on the image yields a telescoping cancellation:
              \[
                  \varphi_{\infty} \left( \sum_{i \geqslant 0} (\varphi-\alpha)^i w \otimes e_i \right) = \sum_{i \geqslant 0} (\varphi-\alpha)^{i+1} w \otimes e_i - \sum_{i \geqslant 0} (\varphi-\alpha)^i w \otimes e_{i-1} = 0
              \]
              (where $e_{-1}=0$). Thus, the image strictly lies in $\ker(\varphi_{\infty})$.
        \item \textbf{Injectivity:} The $e_0$ component of the image sequence is exactly $w \otimes e_0$. If the sum evaluates to zero, this dominant $e_0$ term forces $w=0$.
        \item \textbf{Surjectivity:} Suppose $\eta = \sum_{i \geqslant 0} w_i \otimes e_i \in \ker(\varphi_\infty)$. Setting $\varphi_\infty(\eta) = 0$ implies the recurrence relation $(\varphi-\alpha) w_{i} - w_{i+1} = 0$ across all $i \geqslant 0$. This solves inductively to $w_{i} = (\varphi-\alpha)^i w_0$. Because $\eta$ is a structurally finite sum in $W_\infty$, there must exist some index $N$ where $w_N = 0$. Consequently, $(\varphi-\alpha)^N w_0 = 0$, which precisely dictates that $w_0 \in W_\alpha$. Therefore, $\eta$ is perfectly determined as the exact image of $w_0$.
    \end{itemize}

    To show that $\varphi_{\infty}$ is surjective, we decompose $W = W_{\alpha} \oplus W_{\alpha}^{\perp}$, where $W_{\alpha}^{\perp} = \bigoplus_{\beta \neq \alpha} W_{\beta}$ is the direct sum of all other generalized eigenspaces (or equivalently, the kernel of $Q(\varphi)$ where the minimal polynomial $P(x) = (x-\alpha)^d Q(x)$ and $Q(\alpha) \neq 0$).
    For any generator $w \otimes e_j \in W_\infty$, we construct its preimage under $\varphi_{\infty}$:

    \begin{itemize}
        \item For $w \in W_{\alpha}$, the operator $\varphi - \alpha$ is nilpotent, so the following sum is finite:
              \begin{equation*}
                  \varphi_{\infty}\left( -\sum\limits_{i=j+1}^{\infty} (\varphi-\alpha)^{i-j-1}w\otimes e_{i} \right) = w\otimes e_{j}
              \end{equation*}

        \item For $w \in W_{\alpha}^{\perp}$, the operator $\varphi - \alpha$ is invertible, thus:
              \begin{equation*}
                  \varphi_{\infty}\left( \sum\limits_{i=1}^{j+1}(\varphi-\alpha)^{-i}w\otimes e_{j-i+1} \right) = w\otimes e_{j}
              \end{equation*}
    \end{itemize}
    Thus, $\varphi_{\infty}$ is surjective.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
    For $k\gg 0$, one has a quasi-isomorphism
    \[
        \Psi_{f,\alpha}[1]\ \simeq\ \varinjlim_{m}\Bigl[\,
        Q_m^{\alpha,k}\xrightarrow{s}Q_m^{\alpha,k}\Bigr],
        \qquad
        Q_m^{\alpha,k}:=\frac{N_m^{\alpha,k}}{N_m^{\alpha,-k}}.
    \]
    Here the two-term complex is placed in degrees $(-1,0)$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
    We first calculate that:
    \[
        Q_m^{\alpha,k} = \frac{\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,k}}{\mathcal{N}_m^{\alpha,-k}}
        = \frac{\sum_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s-k} \otimes e_\ell^\alpha}{\sum_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s+k} \otimes e_\ell^\alpha}
        \simeq \bigoplus_{\ell=0}^{m-1} \left( \frac{\mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s-k}}{\mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s+k}} \right) \otimes e_\ell^\alpha
    \]

    We have a \hyperref[construction_of_direct_system_of_N_m_alpha_k]{direct system} of $\D_X[s]$-modules, so it induce the direct system of $\{Q_m^{\alpha,k}\}_{m\ge 1}$.
    And its sequential colimit identifies with the infinite direct sum
    \[
        W_\infty\ :=\ \varinjlim_m Q_m^{\alpha,k}\ \simeq\ \bigoplus_{\ell\ge 0} W\otimes e_\ell^\alpha,
    \]
    where $W = \frac{\mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s-k}}{\mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s+k}}$ is the base module.

    If we view $s$ as an endomorphism of $\frac{\mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s-k}}{\mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s+k}}$,
    first it is $\C$-linear, and it indeed has a minimal polynomial, we get it by multiplying the polynomials $(b_f(s-k+\alpha))^m$ in Lem.~\ref{lemma 10.7}.

    The endomorphism $s:Q_m^{\alpha,k}\to Q_m^{\alpha,k}$ is compatible with the transition maps in the direct system,
    hence induces an endomorphism
    \[
        s_\infty:W_\infty\longrightarrow W_\infty.
    \]

    Under the above identification $W_\infty\simeq \bigoplus_{i\ge 0} W\otimes e_i$,
    this induced map $s_\infty$ is exactly the shift operator $\varphi_\infty$ from Lemma~\ref{lem:the_linear_operator_in_colimit}
    (with eigenvalue $\alpha$). In particular, Lemma~\ref{lem:the_linear_operator_in_colimit} implies that $s_\infty$ is surjective and
    \[
        \ker(s_\infty)\ \simeq\ W_\alpha,
    \]
    the generalized $\alpha$-eigenspace, again, if we view $s$ as an endomorphism of $\frac{\mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s-k}}{\mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s+k}}$.

    So the two-term complex
    \[
        C_\infty^\bullet\ :=\ \Bigl[\,W_\infty\xrightarrow{s_\infty}W_\infty\Bigr],
    \]
    is quasi-isomorphic to $\mathrm{Ker}(s_\infty)[1]$.
    (The functor $[1]$ has different definition in \cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/05RR}{Tag 05RR}]{stacks-project} and \cite[Translation~1.2.8]{Weibel1994HA}, so the editor don't know $[1]$ or $[-1]$ which one is correct, we follow the lecturer's notation.)

    Finally, by the identification $\ker(s_\infty)\simeq W_\alpha$, we link this back to the geometric construction via localization at the maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_\alpha = (s-\alpha)$ of $\mathbb{C}[s]$.

    Recall from the proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:the_linear_operator_in_colimit} that by \textbf{primary decomposition}, $W \simeq W_\alpha \oplus W_\alpha^\perp$,
    both of them are $\C[s]$-modules ($s$-invariant subspace in Linear Algebra), and $W_\alpha^\perp$ is annihilated by some polynomial factor $Q(s)$ such that $Q(\alpha) \neq 0$.

    When we localize the $\mathbb{C}[s]$-module $W$ at $\mathfrak{m}_\alpha$, the polynomial $Q(s) \notin \mathfrak{m}_\alpha$ becomes an invertible unit in the localized ring $\mathbb{C}[s]_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha}$.
    Since it annihilates $W_\alpha^\perp$, this invertibility forces the localized component $(W_\alpha^\perp)_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} = 0$.

    Conversely, any polynomial $H(s) \notin \mathfrak{m}_\alpha$ satisfies $H(\alpha) \neq 0$.
    We can expand $H(s)$ as a polynomial around $s=\alpha$:
    \[
        H(s) = H(\alpha) + (s-\alpha)P(s)
    \]
    for some polynomial $P(s)$.

    Because $s-\alpha$ acts nilpotently on $W_\alpha$, the operator
    \[
        N := -\frac{s-\alpha}{H(\alpha)}P(s)
    \]
    is strictly nilpotent on $W_\alpha$ (i.e., $N^m = 0$ for sufficiently large $m$).

    Then $H(s) / H(\alpha) = 1 - N$, and its inverse is exactly given by the \textbf{finite} geometric series operator
    \[
        \sum_{i=0}^{+\infty} N^i \quad \text{finitely many of } N^i \text{ are non-zero}.
    \]
    This shows $H(s)$ itself acts invertibly on $W_\alpha$. Consequently, localizing at $\mathfrak{m}_\alpha$ does absolutely nothing to $W_\alpha$, yielding $W_\alpha \simeq (W_\alpha)_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha}$.

    Thus, the localization cleanly isolates the $\alpha$-generalized eigenspace:
    \[
        W_\alpha = (W_\alpha)_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} = (W_\alpha)_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} \oplus (W_\alpha^\perp)_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} = W_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} = \left( \frac{\mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s-k}}{\mathcal{D}_X[s] \cdot f^{s+k}} \right)_{\mathfrak{m}_\alpha} = \Psi_{f,\alpha}.
    \]
    Therefore, since $\mathbb{N}$ is a \textbf{directed} set (induced by its partial order $\le$), the corresponding (filtered) direct limit is an exact functor (it commutes with taking cohomology/kernels)\cite[\href{https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/00DB}{Tag 00DB}]{stacks-project}, we get
    \[
        \Psi_{f,\alpha}[1] = W_\alpha[1] = \ker(-s_\infty)[1]\ \simeq\  C_\infty^\bullet = \varinjlim_{m}\Bigl[Q_m^{\alpha,k}\xrightarrow{s}Q_m^{\alpha,k}\Bigr],
    \]
    as claimed.

\end{proof}
